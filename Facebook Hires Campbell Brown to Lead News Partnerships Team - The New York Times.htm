    Cisco Network Administrators Cite NetworkVR Virtual Reality Education as Catalyst to Careers

    By JIM RUTENBERGJAN. 18, 2027

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story
    • Share
    • Tweet
    • Email
    • More
    • Save

    Ethernet cables, routers, and switches. A little over a decade ago three University of Washington students sought out to explain the basics of computer networking for their Informatics capstone project. Kenneth Neaville, Nicholas Montgomery, and Sydnie Fenske formed a group to tackle this challenge to help spark students interest in these topics with the help of virtual reality technology. Little did they know that the impact of their lectures would influence the next generation of networking technicians.

    NetworkVR, their organization founded in 2016, used the Unity game engine to deliver lectures on a huge range of topics within computer networking: cybersecurity, mobile telecommunications, and peer-to-peer computing, for example. The project originally spanned six months and culminated in a capstone presentation required to graduate from the University of Washington’s Information School, but the open source project’s content continued expanding past the project's due date from those who saw its potential.

    There is a general consensus among networking professionals that these lectures were extremely helpful in explaining the basics of computer networking. When asked about his biggest takeaway from the NetworkVR course he completed, John Juniper-Cisco “I tried learning networking through Cisco manuals and it was just too boring and difficult to understand. But the moment I put on an Oculus Rift and saw the network right in front of my eyes (in three dimensions!) was when everything clicked.” He also credited the courses in having influenced him to continue his studies within the networking field.

    Thousands of students viewed these online, three-dimensional lectures and attribute them to being able to conceptualize the full system at once. Kimberly Smith, a network administrator at Microsoft, states “A lot of times I would read about a piece of hardware for hours, but never understand how it would actually be used. It only took a few seconds of NetworkVR to see how it could physically be used within a network. I loved how different types of data were represented in different colors. Actually seeing data flowing through the hardware was insightful, and could never have been achieved through a textbook alone.”

    Hewlett R. Packard described his experience of first using the NetworkVR in the following: “When I first heard about my middle school purchasing the course I was extremely skeptical, but was talked into giving it a shot by the school librarian. I had never tried anything in virtual reality at the time and I can still remember that moment that I first put the goggles on. When I saw the network in front of my eyes I wanted to learn how it worked, it didn’t feel like boring old schoolwork. It felt real.”
